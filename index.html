A Legacy in Clay

"You could almost say that the Cyrus Cylinder is a history of the Middle East in one object... It is the first attempt we know about running a society with different nationalities and faiths—a new kind of statecraft."

- Neil MacGregor, Former Director of the British Museum

The Cyrus Cylinder is a profound artifact from the Achaemenid Period, taking the form of a barrel-shaped cylinder made of baked clay. Created around the 6th century BC as a foundation deposit, it bears a royal inscription written in the Akkadian language using cuneiform script. Today, the artifact exists in two reunited fragments ("A" and "B") and is currently housed in the British Museum. It was unearthed in March 1879 by the Assyro-British archaeologist Hormuzd Rassam, who discovered it during excavations within the foundations of the Ésagila, the main temple of ancient Babylon.

The inscription's historical narrative centers on the Persian king Cyrus the Great and his conquest of Babylon in 539 BC. Across its 45 formulaic lines, the text praises Cyrus while denouncing the deposed Neo-Babylonian king, Nabonidus, as an impious oppressor. The victorious Cyrus is portrayed as the chosen savior appointed by the chief Babylonian god, Marduk, to restore peace and order to the Babylonian people.

The text details Cyrus's peaceful entry into the city, includes a prayer to Marduk for the protection of Cyrus and his son, Cambyses, and extols Cyrus's efforts to repatriate displaced peoples and restore destroyed sanctuaries.

This policy of repatriation has deeply connected the Cylinder to Biblical history. The Bible, specifically the Book of Ezra, records that the Jewish people—who had been exiled to Babylon by the earlier king Nebuchadnezzar—were permitted to return to Jerusalem to rebuild their Temple following an edict from Cyrus. While the exact biblical edicts (recorded in Hebrew and Aramaic) differ in tone from the Cylinder, the artifact's text is widely seen as historical corroboration of Cyrus's tolerant policies toward exiled nations.

The Cyrus Cylinder was dubbed the "first declaration of human rights" by the pre-Revolution Iranian government, a reading prominently advanced by Shah Mohammed Reza Pahlavi, in a 1967 book, The White Revolution of Iran. The Shah identified Cyrus as a key figure in government ideology and associated his government with the Achaemenids. He wrote that "the history of our empire began with the famous declaration of Cyrus, which, for its advocacy of humane principles, justice and liberty, must be considered one of the most remarkable documents in the history of mankind." The Shah described Cyrus as the first ruler in history to give his subjects "freedom of opinion and other basic rights". In 1968, the Shah opened the first United Nations Conference on Human Rights in Tehran by saying that the Cyrus Cylinder was the precursor to the modern Universal Declaration of Human Rights.

In his 1971 Nowruz speech, the Shah declared 1350 AP (1971–1972) "Cyrus the Great Year," launching a grand commemoration of 2,500 years of Persian monarchy. Designed to showcase modern Iran's contributions to world civilization, this vision was physically immortalized through the construction of the iconic Shahyad Tower (now Azadi Tower) in the capital. The Shah explicitly linked his reign to Cyrus, viewing the Achaemenid period as "a moment from the national past that could best serve as a model and a slogan for the imperial society he hoped to create." Beyond this political era, the Cylinder's global resonance endures; in a 2011 TED Talk, former British Museum director Neil MacGregor famously called it "a history of the Middle East in one object," cementing its legacy as a timeless symbol of statecraft and tolerance.

ABOUT THE PROJECT

The Cyrus Cylinder: A Linked Open Data Ecosystem developed for the Information Science and Cultural Heritage course within the Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge (DHDK) Master's program at the University of Bologna, this project bridges an ancient history with modern web architecture. It transcends a traditional static archive by transforming the Cyrus Cylinder—its provenance, textual structure, and profound historical and political legacy—into a dynamic, machine-readable Semantic Knowledge Graph. Beyond structural metadata and Linked Open Data (LOD) methodologies, the project offers an immersive digital narrative. Through a continuous, cinematic user experience, it maps the artifact's temporal journey.

The Theoretical Model

This section presents the theoretical model underlying my ontology. The Knowledge Graph was conceptualized by extracting core entities, relations, and data properties directly from our TEI/XML markup of the Wikipedia entry on the Cyrus Cylinder. However, to ensure a comprehensive domain representation, the model intentionally extends beyond this primary corpus to integrate external entities, capturing a broader historical, political, and cultural context.

The Conceptual Model

Building upon the theoretical framework, the Conceptual Model maps my extracted entities and relationships at a higher level of abstraction through a strategic multi-ontology approach. While CIDOC-CRM and FRBRoo serve as the primary backbone to provide robust semantics for modeling historical artifacts, events, and complex textual lifecycles, Schema.org is integrated as a supplementary vocabulary. This integration successfully bridges the gap created by the structural rigidities of cultural heritage ontologies, allowing for a more flexible and comprehensive representation of generic or contemporary web relationships and external entities within our Linked Open Data ecosystem.

XML to HTML Transformation Methodology

This table displays the semantic relationships extracted from the TEI/XML file. The primary purpose of XML/TEI is to describe and exchange metadata, but it does not address presentational aspects for web browsers. To make the XML document browsable and accessible to a wider audience, it must be transformed into an HTML document. For this project, XSLT (eXtensible Stylesheet Language Transformations), a declarative language, was used to convert the XML nodes into a structured HTML table.

Behind the scenes, the relationships are logically organized by entity types such as Persons, Places, and Organizations etc. To ensure the knowledge graph is truly user-friendly, the code automatically translates complex machine IDs and ontology prefixes into real, readable names. By seamlessly blending our internal XML data with external authority records, this approach provides a clear and easy-to-read view of the project's semantic network.

RDF Production

The subsequent phase translated my conceptual model into a machine-readable Linked Open Data architecture by serializing the extracted entities and relationships into an RDF graph using Turtle (.ttl) syntax. To ensure robustness and interoperability, this production was driven by a systematic URI minting strategy that defined custom namespaces (such as data: and ext:) for all domain-specific entities. Furthermore, to prevent my Knowledge Graph from remaining an isolated silo, I actively bridged it to the global Semantic Web by implementing owl:sameAs, crm:P1_is_identified_by and skos:exactMatch properties, precisely reconciling our internal entities with major international authority records including Wikidata, VIAF, and Pleiades.
